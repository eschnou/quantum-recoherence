\title{Where do the other worlds go?\\
  A toy-model test of Born-rule filtering in a local Ising chain}

\author{Laurent Eschenauer}
\affiliation{Independent Researcher}
\email{laurent@eschenauer.be}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
Strasberg and collaborators showed, in random matrix models, that
Born-atypical quantum histories recohere while Born-typical ones remain
decoherent.  We test this in a 9-qubit transverse-field Ising chain
($D = 512$): histories with Born-typical frequencies remain $3.1\times$ more
decoherent than those at the branch-counting peak, and individual-branch
tracking confirms that survivors cluster at Born-typical frequencies.  The effect requires scrambling dynamics and is robust across
system sizes ($D = 128$--$1024$) and projector choices.
When 8~qubits are split into two spatial groups with independent projectors,
Born filtering operates simultaneously at each level of coarse-graining.
At the branch level, coarse-grained worlds can remain decoherent even after
all their fine-grained constituents have recohered.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

Quantum mechanics has a puzzle at its core, and it is surprisingly easy to state.

The Schr\"odinger equation is deterministic and linear. If you know the state of a
system right now, you can compute its state at any future time---no randomness, no
choices, no collapse. And yet, every time we open the box and look at the cat, we
see one definite outcome. The math says the cat is in a superposition of alive and
dead. Our eyes say it is one or the other.

This is the measurement problem, and it has been with us since the 1920s. There is
no shortage of proposed solutions. The Copenhagen interpretation says the wave
function collapses upon measurement, but never quite explains what counts as a
measurement. Bohmian mechanics adds hidden variables. Objective collapse theories
add new physics. And then there is Everett's proposal~\cite{Everett1957}: nothing
collapses at all. The Schr\"odinger equation is the whole story. What we experience
as ``collapse'' is the universe splitting into branches---every possible outcome
happens, each in its own branch of reality, and we simply find ourselves in one of
them.

The Everett picture is elegant, but it raises its own question. If every outcome
happens, why do we observe outcomes with the frequencies predicted by the Born rule?
If there is one branch where the electron goes left and one where it goes right,
and both are equally real, why do we not see 50/50 statistics regardless of the
quantum state?

This is sometimes called the \emph{probability problem} of the many-worlds
interpretation, and it is the subject of a large literature.
Deutsch~\cite{Deutsch1999} gave the first decision-theoretic argument:
a rational agent in a branching universe \emph{should} weight branches by their
squared amplitudes. Wallace~\cite{Wallace2012} refined and extended the proof;
Carroll~\cite{Carroll2019} offers an accessible account and argues,
with Sebens, that Born's rule follows from self-locating uncertainty at the
moment of branching.
Zurek~\cite{Zurek2005} pursued a different strategy, deriving Born's rule from
environment-assisted invariance (``envariance'') without decision theory.
These approaches are persuasive to some and unconvincing to others. What they share
is that they introduce additional postulates---rationality axioms or
symmetry principles---beyond the Schr\"odinger equation itself.

Recently, Strasberg and collaborators~\cite{Strasberg2024, StrasbergSchindler2024,
Strasberg2025} have explored a different route. In a finite-dimensional Hilbert space, the histories of a
quantum system cannot all remain decoherent as the number of branching events grows.
There are more histories than dimensions, so branches must start overlapping.
In random matrix toy models, they reported that this overlap is not uniform: the
histories whose frequencies match the Born rule remain decoherent, while the others
do not. Whether this filtering pattern is a generic feature of finite-dimensional
quantum mechanics or specific to the models studied remains an open question.

Strasberg's results are established for random matrix Hamiltonians and related
asymptotic arguments. We ask: does the same qualitative behavior appear in a
concrete, physically motivated Hamiltonian? We take a 9-qubit transverse-field Ising
chain---a nearest-neighbor spin model near its critical point---and check.

\section{The Puzzle: All Branches Exist, but the Statistics Are Wrong}
\label{sec:puzzle}
% ============================================================================

Take a loaded coin: 75\% heads, 25\% tails. We know what probability means here.
Flip it many times, and about 75\% of the flips will come up heads. That is what
we observe. That is what we mean by the coin being ``loaded.''

Now flip it once in the Everett picture. Both outcomes happen. There are two
branches: one where you got heads, one where you got tails. Both are real. Both
exist. So what does ``75\% probability'' even mean, if both outcomes occur with
certainty?

Flip again. Now there are four branches: HH, HT, TH, TT. All four exist. Of
these, one has 0 heads, two have 1 head, and one has 2 heads. If you just count
branches, the most common outcome is 50\% heads---not 75\%.

After three flips, there are 8 branches. The branch-counting peak is still at
50\% heads. After ten flips, there are 1024 branches, and $\binom{10}{5} = 252$
of them have exactly 50\% heads, while only $\binom{10}{7} = 120$ have 70\% heads.
The more you flip, the worse it gets: branch counting says 50\%, but we observe
75\%.

This is the probability problem of the many-worlds interpretation. If every branch
is equally real, what does probability mean? And why do we see the loaded-coin
statistics instead of the branch-counting statistics?

\section{Background: Recoherence in Finite Hilbert Space}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================================

A quantum system with $m$ qubits lives in a Hilbert space of dimension $D = 2^m$.
Think of this as a pond with a fixed surface area. Each branch of the wave function
is a lily pad. After $L$ coin flips, there are $2^L$ lily pads. When $L$ is small,
there is plenty of room---each pad has its own patch of water, and they do not
interfere. But the pond is finite. When $2^L > D$, the pads are competing for the
same surface. They crowd, overlap, and shade each other out. Not all can survive
as distinct plants.

In quantum language, branches that ``overlap'' are no longer orthogonal. Their
quantum states point in nearly the same direction, which means they cannot be
distinguished by any measurement. They have lost their identity as separate
worlds. This language draws on the decoherent (or consistent) histories
framework~\cite{Griffiths1984, GellMannHartle1990}, in which a set of histories
counts as ``classical'' only when their mutual overlaps are negligible.
Following Strasberg~\cite{Strasberg2025}, we call the loss of this condition
\emph{recoherence}---the opposite of decoherence.

Strasberg and collaborators~\cite{StrasbergSchindler2024, Strasberg2025}
reported that, in their random matrix models, this competition is not democratic.
The branches that get shaded out are not random.
They are the ones near 50\% heads---the branch-counting peak. The branches near
75\% heads---the Born frequency---remain more nearly decoherent.

\subsection{Measuring Decoherence}

To make this precise, we need a way to measure how much two branches overlap.

We group branches by their frequency---the total number of heads $n_1$ out of $L$
flips. The frequency-class state $|\psi(n_1)\rangle$ is the quantum state
obtained by adding up all branches that got exactly $n_1$ heads:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:freq-class}
  |\psi(n_1)\rangle = \sum_{\mathbf{x}:\, n_1(\mathbf{x}) = n_1}
    \Pi_{x_L} U_L \cdots \Pi_{x_1} U_1 |\Psi_0\rangle,
\end{equation}
where $U_k$ is the evolution between flips and $\Pi_{x_k}$ picks out the
heads-or-tails outcome at flip $k$.

The \emph{recoherence parameter}
$\varepsilon$~\cite{StrasbergSchindler2024} measures how much a frequency class
overlaps with any other frequency class---a score from 0 to 1:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:epsilon}
  \varepsilon(n_1) = \max_{n_1' \neq n_1}
    \frac{|\langle\psi(n_1')|\psi(n_1)\rangle|}
         {\sqrt{\langle\psi(n_1)|\psi(n_1)\rangle \cdot \langle\psi(n_1')|\psi(n_1')\rangle}}.
\end{equation}
At $\varepsilon = 0$, the frequency class is fully decoherent (orthogonal to all
others). At $\varepsilon = 1$, it is fully recoherent (indistinguishable from
another class).

\subsection{The Prediction}

In their random matrix toy models, Strasberg and collaborators found that, as the
number of flips $L$ grows:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Frequency classes near 75\% heads have low $\varepsilon$---they stay decoherent,
  \item frequency classes near 50\% heads have high $\varepsilon$---they blur together,
  \item the gap grows with $L$.
\end{itemize}
The pond is too small for all the lily pads near 50\%. They shade each other out.
The ones near 75\% remain more nearly decoherent.

\section{Numerical Experiment}
\label{sec:experiment}
% ============================================================================

These results are established for random matrix Hamiltonians and in asymptotic
limits.  Whether the same filtering pattern survives in a small, structured,
physically motivated system is untested.  We check.

We simulate repeated coarse-grained measurements on a 9-qubit Ising chain and
analyze the decoherence structure of the resulting
branches.\footnote{All simulation code is available at
\url{https://github.com/eschnou/quantum-recoherence}.}

The setup can be thought of as a loaded quantum coin flip repeated $L$ times.
The coin comes up heads 75\% of the time. In the many-worlds picture, every
outcome sequence occurs as a branch. We track all $2^L$ branches and compute the
recoherence parameter $\varepsilon$ for each, asking: which branches remain
approximately decoherent, and which lose their identity through overlap with
neighbors?

\subsection{Setup: The Ising Chain}
\label{sec:setup}

We use 9 qubits arranged in a line, interacting through the transverse-field Ising
Hamiltonian:
\begin{equation}
  H = \frac{J}{2} \sum_{i=0}^{7} Z_i Z_{i+1}
    + h_x \sum_{i=0}^{8} X_i,
\end{equation}
with parameters $J/2 = 0.5$ and $h_x = 0.9045$, placing the system near the
quantum critical point ($h_x/J \approx 0.9$). In this regime, the nearest-neighbor
couplings generate significant entanglement and spreading of correlations. The
Hilbert space has dimension $D = 2^9 = 512$.

Each ``coin flip'' is not a physical measurement but an analytical decomposition:
let the qubits evolve under $H$ for one time step, then project the state onto
two orthogonal subspaces. Our question is: are at least
4 of the 9 qubits in state $|1\rangle$? If yes, we call it ``heads.'' If no,
``tails.'' Since 382 of the 512 basis states have Hamming weight $\geq 4$, the
coin is loaded: $p_\text{heads} = d_1/D = 382/512 \approx 75\%$, where $d_1$ is
the rank of the ``heads'' projector. In Strasberg's framework, this ratio $d_1/D$
defines the Born-typical frequency: it is the single-trial Born probability
averaged over Haar-random initial states.

\subsection{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

Each evolution step applies $U = e^{-iH\Delta t}$ with $\Delta t = 1.2$,
computed via exact matrix exponentiation. The time step was chosen large enough
for the dynamics to spread correlations across the chain between projections;
smaller values ($\Delta t \lesssim 0.5$) produce weaker filtering because the
state has not evolved far from the previous projection subspace.

Initial states are drawn Haar-randomly on the full $D$-dimensional Hilbert space.
All frequency-class results are averaged over 10 independent random seeds; error
bars show $\pm 1\sigma$.

Branch-level decoherence counts use the operational threshold
$\varepsilon < 0.3$. This threshold is a conventional choice; the qualitative
conclusion---that survivors cluster at Born-typical frequencies---holds for
thresholds in the range $[0.1, 0.5]$, though the absolute count of
``decoherent'' branches varies (see Appendix~\ref{sec:threshold} for the full
threshold sensitivity analysis).
A non-scrambling control (product rotation
$U_\text{prod} = e^{-i\theta X}{}^{\otimes m}$ at matched $p_1$) produces no
stable filtering, confirming that scrambling dynamics are required
(Appendix~\ref{sec:product-control}).

\subsection{Frequency-Class Results}
\label{sec:freq-results}

We first analyze frequency classes (groups of branches with the same number of
heads) at $L = 15$, averaged over 10 Haar-random initial states.

Figure~\ref{fig:overlay} shows the result. The frequency classes near 75\% heads
(the Born prediction) have low $\varepsilon$---they remain approximately
decoherent. The classes near 50\% heads (the branch-counting peak) have
$\varepsilon$ approximately three times higher---they overlap significantly with
neighboring classes.

As a sanity check, we also run Strasberg's own random matrix model at the same
dimension and coin loading. The two curves show the same qualitative pattern---high
$\varepsilon$ at combinatorial-peak frequencies, low $\varepsilon$ near the Born
frequency.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../results/overlay.png}
  \caption{Recoherence parameter $\varepsilon(n_1/L)$ for the 9-qubit
    transverse-field Ising chain (red, exact matrix exponentiation) and
    Strasberg's random matrix model (blue) at $D = 512$, $L = 15$,
    $p_1 = 0.746$. Averaged over 10 Haar-random initial states; shaded regions
    show $\pm 1\sigma$. Both curves dip near the Born frequency (red dashed)
    and rise toward the combinatorial center (green dotted), showing the same

  \label{fig:overlay}
\end{figure}

The result is robust. All 10 seeds show the same qualitative shape. The standard
deviation of $\varepsilon$ across seeds is small relative to the Born--combinatorial
gap, indicating that the effect is a property of the Hamiltonian, not of special
initial states. The effect also persists across system sizes ($D = 128$ to $1024$)
and alternative coarse-grainings; see Appendix~\ref{sec:robustness} for the full
scaling and projector analysis.

\subsection{Branch-Level Analysis}
\label{sec:branches}

So far we have grouped branches by their frequency (how many heads total). But we
can go deeper: track every single branch individually. Each specific sequence of
heads and tails---like HHTHTTHHTTHH---is one branch, one possible history of the
coin. We compute $\varepsilon$ for each one: how much does this branch overlap with
any other?

Figure~\ref{fig:branch-death} shows what happens as we increase the number of
flips. At $L = 4$ (16 branches, 512 dimensions), the pond is nearly empty---almost
every branch is a distinct world. At $L = 9$ (512 branches, 512 dimensions), the
pond is full. At $L = 12$ (4096 branches, 512 dimensions), it is eight times
oversubscribed. Only 451 branches remain approximately decoherent
($\varepsilon < 0.3$). By $L = 13$ (8192 branches), 565 survive---slightly
above $D$, since the threshold $\varepsilon < 0.3$ allows partial overlap.

The survivors cluster at Born-typical frequencies.

    threshold $\varepsilon = 0.3$ is shown as a gray dashed line. At $L = 13$,
    8192 branches compete for 512 dimensions; only 565 satisfy
    $\varepsilon < 0.3$, clustered near the Born frequency (red dashed).}
  \label{fig:branch-death}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:census} makes the census explicit. The total number of branches
grows exponentially ($2^L$), but the growth of decoherent branches slows
dramatically around $D = 512$. The dimension $D$ is not a hard ceiling---approximate
decoherence ($\varepsilon < 0.3$) allows more than $D$ coexisting branches---but
it sets the scale at which the competition intensifies.

  \label{fig:census}
\end{figure}

The frequency distribution of survivors matches a binomial with Born probability
$p_1 = 0.746$, not the combinatorial distribution from naive branch counting.

\subsection{The All-Zeros and All-Ones Asymmetry}
\label{sec:asymmetry}

Two extreme branches tell the story most vividly.

The \emph{all-tails} branch---tails on every single flip. This is the most
anti-Born history possible. Its weight is $2 \times 10^{-8}$ (essentially zero),
and its $\varepsilon = 0.77$---it overlaps heavily with neighboring branches and
fails the approximate decoherence criterion by a wide margin.

The \emph{all-heads} branch---heads every time. There is only one such history
out of 4096, so by branch counting it is just as rare as all-tails. But the coin is
loaded 75\% heads, so this outcome is not absurd. Its weight is 0.025
(a million times larger than all-tails), and its $\varepsilon = 0.20$---it
remains approximately decoherent. It has no siblings with the same frequency to
compete with, so it retains its share of the Hilbert space.

In this model, anti-Born branches do not just have low weight. They also fail the
approximate decoherence criterion used to identify distinct record-bearing
histories.

\subsection{Individual Branches}
\label{sec:one-reality}

Finally, we pick four individual branches and trace their evolution step by step
(Figure~\ref{fig:one-reality}).

Each row shows one branch's outcome sequence, how its running frequency $n_1(k)/k$
evolves flip by flip, and its weight $\|\psi(\mathbf{x},k)\|^2$ at each step.
The Born-typical branch sees its frequency converge to 75\% and retains significant
weight. The anti-Born branches drift toward 50\% and their weight drops by many
orders of magnitude.

\section{Multi-Scale Born Filtering}
\label{sec:multiscale}
% ============================================================================

We now ask whether the filtering operates simultaneously at multiple spatial
scales of coarse-graining on the same Hilbert space.

We split 8~qubits ($D = 256$) into two groups of 4 (left: qubits 0--3, right:
4--7) and apply independent Hamming-weight projectors ($\text{HW} \geq 2$) to
each group at every time step.  For example, if the left group has 3 qubits in
$|1\rangle$ and the right group has 1, the joint outcome is $(1, 0)$---left
fires, right does not.  The four joint outcomes $(00, 01, 10, 11)$ partition
$\mathcal{H}$ into orthogonal subspaces.  From these we construct two scales
of binary projectors:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Scale~1}: each group independently
    ($p_1 = 11/16 = 0.688$),
  \item \textbf{Scale~2}: ``at least one group shows 1,'' i.e.\
    $\Pi_2 = I - \Pi_{0,L} \otimes \Pi_{0,R}$
    ($p_1 = 231/256 = 0.902$).
\end{itemize}
Both are proper orthogonal projectors; the scale-2 Born probability is
determined by rank$(\Pi_2) = 231$, independently of whether the observer knows
the spatial decomposition.

Figure~\ref{fig:spatial-overlay} shows the frequency-class $\varepsilon$ at
both scales.  Each curve dips near its respective Born frequency
($p_1 = 0.69$ at scale~1, $p_1 = 0.90$ at scale~2), with the
larger projector asymmetry at scale~2 producing a stronger gap
($9\times$ vs.\ $2\times$ at $L = 20$).
Figure~\ref{fig:spatial-emergence} confirms that the dip develops
progressively with $L$ at both scales.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../results/spatial_overlay.png}
  \caption{$\varepsilon(n/L)$ at two spatial scales on an 8-qubit Ising chain
    ($D = 256$, $L = 20$, 10 Haar-random seeds).  Scale~1 curves (red, orange)
    dip near $p_1 = 0.69$; scale~2 (blue) dips near $p_1 = 0.90$.
    Shading: $\pm 1\sigma$.}

\end{figure}

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../results/spatial_emergence.png}
  \caption{Emergence of the filtering dip at two spatial scales as $L$ grows.
    Top: scale~1 (left group, $p_1 = 0.69$).
    Bottom: scale~2 (either group, $p_1 = 0.90$).
    Averaged over 5 seeds.}
  \label{fig:spatial-emergence}
\end{figure*}

The scale-2 observer perceives $p_1 = 231/256$ as a fundamental property of
their projector.  The factorization into spatial subgroups---$p_2 = 1 -
(1 - p_{1,L})(1 - p_{1,R})$---is invisible at the coarser scale, just as
molecular-scale Born probabilities do not reveal the underlying atomic
projectors.

\subsection{Emergent Decoherence}
\label{sec:emergent-decoherence}

The two scales have different branching rates.  The fine-grained (scale-1)
description has four outcomes per step, producing $4^L$ branches; the
coarse-grained (scale-2) description has two, producing $2^L$.  Both compete
for the same $D = 256$ dimensions, but the finer description exhausts them
faster.

We track three nesting metrics across $L$ (averaged over 5--20 seeds,
$\varepsilon < 0.3$).  S1/S2 denote the mean number of decoherent branches
at each scale.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Containment}: fraction of scale-1 survivors inside a surviving
    scale-2 branch.
  \item \emph{Emergence}: fraction of scale-2 survivors whose scale-1
    constituents are \emph{all} dead.
  \item \emph{Leak}: fraction of dead scale-2 branches containing any
    scale-1 survivor.
\end{itemize}

\smallskip
\noindent
\begin{tabular}{rrrrccc}
  \toprule
  $L$ & $4^L$ & S1 & S2 & Contain.\ & Emerg.\ & Leak \\
  \midrule
  4 &     256 &  42 &  8 & 98\% &  45\% & 4\% \\
  5 &   1\,024 &  25 & 10 & 98\% &  57\% & 1\% \\
  6 &   4\,096 &   6 & 10 & 98\% &  74\% & 0\% \\
  7 &  16\,384 & $<\!1$ & 11 & 100\% &  95\% & 0\% \\
  8 &  65\,536 &   0 & 11 &  --- & 100\% & 0\% \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\smallskip

Containment is near-perfect; leakage is near-zero.  Decoherence flows from
fine to coarse, never the reverse.

Figure~\ref{fig:spatial-nesting} makes the structure visible.  Each row is
one scale-2 branch; the left panel shows its $\varepsilon$ (blue = decoherent,
red = dead), and the right panel shows every scale-1 branch inside it
(blue dots = decoherent, gray = dead).  The blue dots appear \emph{only}
inside blue-bar rows: no dead scale-2 branch contains a surviving scale-1
branch.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../results/spatial_nesting.png}
  \caption{Nesting at $L = 6$ (4096 scale-1 branches, 64 scale-2 branches,
    $D = 256$).  Left: scale-2 $\varepsilon$ per branch (blue = decoherent,
    red = dead).  Right: scale-1 members within each scale-2 branch (blue
    dots = decoherent).  All 13 scale-1 survivors live inside decoherent
    scale-2 branches; several decoherent scale-2 branches contain no
    surviving scale-1 members (emergent decoherence).}
  \label{fig:spatial-nesting}
\end{figure*}

The emergence column is the main result.  By $L = 8$, scale~1 is
entirely dead: zero out of $65\,536$ branches satisfy $\varepsilon < 0.3$.
Yet $\sim\!10$ scale-2 branches remain decoherent---each a coherent
sum of individually dead scale-1 branches.  The coarse-grained world
supports Born-typical frequencies even though none of its microscopic
constituents retain a separate identity.

The mechanism is dimensional: four outcomes per step produce $4^L$ branches,
oversubscribing $D = 256$ by $256\times$ at $L = 8$; two outcomes per step
produce $2^L$, reaching only $1\times$.  The coarser observer creates fewer
branches and survives longer---not despite seeing less, but because of it.

\section{Discussion: Why Classicality Lives at the Top}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

The emergence result inverts a common intuition.  We normally think of the
macroscopic world as a coarse-grained approximation of a sharper microscopic
reality.  The nesting table says otherwise: by $L = 8$, the fine-grained
description has zero decoherent branches out of $65\,536$.  Every scale-1
history has recohered.  Yet $\sim\!10$ scale-2 branches remain decoherent,
each a superposition of thousands of individually dead scale-1 histories.
In the sense that matters for classical reality (distinct histories, stable
records, Born-rule statistics), the macroscopic description is not a degraded
version of the microscopic one.  It is the level at which distinguishable
worlds exist.

The mechanism is dimensional: fine-grained branching produces $4^L$ histories,
exhausting the Hilbert space before coarse-grained branching ($2^L$) does.
The containment and leakage metrics confirm that decoherence flows strictly
from fine to coarse, never the reverse.  A hypothetical observer tracking
physics at full microscopic resolution would find no consistent classical
history.  Only a coarse-grained observer, one who compresses microstates into
macroscopic readings, creates few enough branches to maintain decoherence.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================================

This work has clear limitations.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Small system.} We test $D = 128$ to $D = 1024$ (7--10 qubits).
    Appendix~\ref{sec:scaling} shows the filtering gap grows with $D$ at matched
    projector asymmetry, consistent with Strasberg's $D^{-\alpha}$
    prediction~\cite{Strasberg2024}, but the accessible range spans less than one
    order of magnitude. Establishing the asymptotic scaling requires larger systems.

  \item \textbf{Classical simulation only.} We simulate the full state vector on a
    classical computer. This limits us to small systems. A quantum computer could
    in principle probe larger Hilbert spaces, but the analysis requires tracking
    exponentially many branches, which is inherently hard.

  \item \textbf{Single Hamiltonian family.} We test only the transverse-field Ising
    chain. The 1D transverse-field Ising model is formally integrable (via
    Jordan--Wigner), unlike the chaotic random matrix models used by Strasberg.
    However, at 9~sites with open boundary conditions near the critical point,
    finite-size effects introduce sufficient dynamical complexity for the
    filtering to appear.  Whether a genuinely non-integrable Hamiltonian
    (e.g., adding a longitudinal field $h_z Z$) would strengthen the effect
    is an open question and a natural extension of this work.

  \item \textbf{Projector dependence partially explored.} We test four projectors
    (Appendix~\ref{sec:projectors}): three Hamming-weight thresholds and a
    spatially biased projector. All asymmetric projectors ($p_1 \neq 0.5$)
    show filtering, including the spatial projector that is structurally unlike
    Hamming weight. However, the filtering strength depends primarily on the
    projector asymmetry $|p_1 - 1/2|$, not the projector type.
    When $p_1 = 0.5$, Born and combinatorial peaks coincide and no filtering
    occurs---as predicted, but limiting the range of independent tests.
    We have not tested projectors selected by physical symmetries
    (e.g., gauge invariance).

  \item \textbf{Time-step dependence.} The Born--combinatorial gap depends on
    $\Delta t$, requiring sufficient evolution between projections for the
    dynamics to spread correlations. The reported $3.1\times$ gap is specific to
    $\Delta t = 1.2$.

  \item \textbf{No environment.} Real decoherence involves tracing out an
    environment. Our system has no environment---the 9 qubits are the whole
    universe. This is intentional (it matches Strasberg's formalism, where the
    system itself is the recorder), but it is a simplification.

  \item \textbf{Multi-scale analysis limited to two spatial levels.} We test
    one spatial coarse-graining step (two 4-qubit groups $\to$ one joint
    projector) on 8~qubits ($D = 256$).  The argument predicts that deeper
    hierarchies (e.g., groups of groups) should also show Born filtering, but
    verifying this requires larger systems.  The 8-qubit system is smaller than
    the 9-qubit system used for the single-scale analysis, limiting the
    filtering strength at scale~1.
\end{itemize}


% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================================

In transverse-field Ising chains from 7 to 10 qubits ($D = 128$ to $1024$),
the recoherence parameter $\varepsilon$ dips near the Born frequency, with
filtering gaps ranging from $1.9\times$ to $6.1\times$ depending on system size
and projector asymmetry. The effect is not specific to a single coarse-graining:
it appears for Hamming-weight projectors at multiple thresholds and for a
spatially biased projector that examines only a subsystem. The filtering
strength scales primarily with the projector asymmetry $|p_1 - 1/2|$ and
grows with Hilbert space dimension at fixed asymmetry.
A non-scrambling control confirms that projector asymmetry alone is
insufficient: scrambling dynamics are required (Appendix~\ref{sec:product-control}).
At the individual-branch level, the finite Hilbert space preferentially preserves
Born-typical branches while anti-Born branches lose approximate decoherence.
This is consistent with Strasberg's analytical results and extends the qualitative
picture from random matrices to a local, physically motivated Hamiltonian.

The multi-scale analysis reveals that Born filtering operates simultaneously
at multiple spatial scales.  When 8~qubits are split into two groups with
independent Hamming-weight projectors, the single-group level ($p_1 = 0.69$)
and the coarser ``at least one group active'' level ($p_1 = 0.90$) each show
Born filtering independently, with the larger projector asymmetry at the coarser
level producing stronger filtering ($9\times$ vs $2\times$ gap).  The coarser
Born probability derives from the group-level projector ranks, but this
decomposition is invisible to the coarser observer.

A natural next step is to test the filtering mechanism in a lattice gauge theory.
In a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ or $U(1)$ lattice gauge model, the Hilbert space factorizes
into gauge-invariant sectors (charge sectors, flux sectors) that provide a
physically dictated projector hierarchy---unlike the Hamming-weight projectors
chosen by hand here.  The coarse-graining from link variables to plaquette
observables to Wilson loops would give a concrete multi-scale structure where
each level's projectors are fixed by the gauge symmetry.  If Born filtering
survives in that setting, the case for a physically grounded mechanism becomes
considerably stronger.

\section{Robustness and Scaling}
\label{sec:robustness}
% ============================================================================

We present three robustness checks that address natural concerns about the
generality of the filtering pattern reported in Sec.~\ref{sec:freq-results}:
scaling with Hilbert space dimension, dependence on the coarse-graining
projector, and sensitivity to the decoherence threshold.

\subsection{Scaling with System Size}
\label{sec:scaling}

We repeat the frequency-class analysis for Ising chains of 7, 8, 9, and 10
qubits ($D = 128$, 256, 512, 1024), varying the Hamming-weight threshold to
explore different values of $p_1$.

Figure~\ref{fig:scaling-profiles} shows the $\varepsilon$ profile at the
largest $L$ tested for each configuration. The qualitative shape---a dip near
the Born frequency and a rise toward the combinatorial peak---is present across
all system sizes and all asymmetric projectors ($p_1 \neq 0.5$).

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../results/scaling_profiles.png}
  \caption{Recoherence parameter $\varepsilon(n_1/L)$ at the largest $L$ tested
    for six configurations spanning $D = 128$ to $1024$. Red dashed line marks the
    Born frequency $p_1$; green dotted line marks the combinatorial peak at 0.5.
    Each curve is averaged over 10 Haar-random initial states; shading shows
    $\pm 1\sigma$. The filtering dip near the Born frequency appears at every
    system size.}
  \label{fig:scaling-profiles}
\end{figure*}

The Born--combinatorial gap is governed by two factors
(Fig.~\ref{fig:scaling-gap}). The dominant factor is the projector asymmetry
$|p_1 - 1/2|$: configurations with $p_1$ close to 0.5 show only marginal
filtering (gap $\approx 1.5\times$), while strongly asymmetric projectors
($p_1 > 0.8$) produce gaps exceeding $6\times$. This is expected: when
$p_1 = 0.5$, the Born and combinatorial peaks coincide, leaving nothing to
filter. The second factor is system size: at comparable $p_1$, larger $D$
yields a larger gap. For example, at $p_1 \approx 0.77$--$0.83$, the gap
increases from $2.3\times$ ($D = 128$) to $3.1\times$ ($D = 512$) to
$6.1\times$ ($D = 1024$).

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../results/scaling_vs_D.png}
  \caption{Left: Born--combinatorial gap at the largest $L$ tested, plotted
    against projector asymmetry $|p_1 - 1/2|$. Marker color indicates Hilbert
    space dimension $D$. At comparable asymmetry, larger $D$ yields a larger gap.
    Right: gap growth with $L$ for asymmetric projectors only ($p_1 > 0.6$),
    showing monotonic increase at all system sizes.}
  \label{fig:scaling-gap}
\end{figure*}

The branch-level analysis in Sec.~\ref{sec:branches} uses the operational

\begin{figure*}[t]

\subsection{Non-Scrambling Control}
\label{sec:product-control}

We replace the Ising Hamiltonian with a product rotation
$U_{\text{prod}} = e^{-i\theta X}{}^{\otimes m}$ ($\theta = h_x \Delta t$),
which generates no entanglement.  Projectors, initial states, and $p_1$ are
unchanged.  The product rotation produces no stable filtering: the
Born--combinatorial gap collapses to $1.3\times$ and $\varepsilon_\text{Born}
\approx 0.5$ at all $L$ (Figure~\ref{fig:product-control}), confirming that
scrambling is a necessary ingredient.
